And Now for Something Completely Different

Case 5: statistics in the courtroom

  • You will pretend that you have been hired as an expert witness in a court case;
  • In teams, you will conduct an analysis and write an expert witness report for a non-technical audience (judge, jury, lawyers);
  • Instead of giving a slide presentation, you will then “take the stand” and testify about your report.

Expert witnesses in general

  • The American system is adversarial;
  • There is prosecution and defense, and each may hire their own experts;
  • While one side or the other hires the expert, they are not supposed to be an advocate for that side;
  • A lawyer hires an expert to conduct a neutral analysis:
    • If the analysis doesn’t help their case, they bury it and hire someone else;
    • If it does help, the expert might be deposed or testify at trial (very lucrative);
  • The expert is subjected to questions under oath about their background, credentials, report, and the report of other expert witnesses;
  • If the expert testifies, everything they did is discoverable. So, if they conduct two analyses, one incriminating and one exculpatory, but then bury one…that’s bad.

Statistical experts in particular

A statistician (often a PhD academic, but not always) might be hired to…

  • explain statistical concepts to the non-expert jury;
  • respond to opposing experts;
  • evaluate statistical evidence or analysis at issue in the litigation;
  • quantify uncertainty, estimate likelihood, evaluate causality;
  • estimate damages.

Case 5

The case

In 2012, the Obama Justice Department charged the sheriff of Alamance County NC with discriminating against Latino drivers at traffic stops.

Statistical experts were hired to assess the strength of evidence for three kinds of targeting:

  • The sheriff’s office placed checkpoints at sites more likely to encounter Latinos;
  • Checkpoint screenings led to targeted arrests of Latinos;
  • Latinos were more likely to be stopped for minor violations in non-checkpoint situations.

Data sources include Alamance County Sheriff’s Office, the U.S. Census Bureau, and the North Carolina state government.

Your task: phase one

Your client has sent you a horrific pile of files containing the traffic stop records of the sheriff’s office from 2009 - 2012:

  • Mop up the mess;
  • Write an expert witness report;
  • Reach whatever conclusion seems correct to you.

Judge, jury, and attorneys are the audience. Not statisticians.

Your task: phase two

After you submit the report, we will review it, and then your team will “take the stand” for 25 minutes:

  • An “attorney” sympathetic to your conclusion will examine you;
  • An “attorney” hostile to your conclusion will cross-examine you.

The “attorneys” will come from Duke’s Mock Trial team.

Timeline (Section 2)

This Case is not about technique, so no content lectures. Furthermore, we’re asking you to write an airtight report on an accelerated timeline. So:

  • Friday April 3: lab as usual;
  • Monday April 6: in-class work period;
  • Wednesday April 8: Q&A with Dr. David Banks, who has been an expert witness;
  • Friday April 10: lab as usual;
  • Monday April 13: intro Case 6, in-class work period and final report due;
  • Wednesday April 15: Presentation (3 teams);
  • Monday April 20: Presentation (3 teams);
  • Wednesday April 22: Presentation (3 teams).

Words of caution

  • This is not about technique. Your audience (judge, jury, and lawyers) know virtually nothing about statistics. Stick to simple things you can actually explain!
  • You may not have written every part of your team’s report, but your name is on it. Be prepared to explain and defend everything in it;
  • You are a statistical expert witness. Don’t opine about things beyond your expertise;
  • You wouldn’t wear sweatpants to court, would you?

The Sally Clark case

Background

  • Sally Clark (1964 - 2007): english solicitor;
  • 1996: first son died in bed at 11 weeks;
  • 1998: second son died in bed at 8 weeks;
  • Clark arrested on suspicion of murder;
  • Defense claimed it was SIDS (sudden infant death syndrome).

Expert testimony

  • Sir Roy Meadow (1933 - );
  • Pediatrician specializing in “Munchausen syndrome by proxy;”
  • Gave testimony that there is a “1 in 73,000,000” chance of two SIDS deaths in the same family;
  • A second expert was not engaged to push back on this.

1 in 73,000,000?

Behold:

  • In the 1990s, the incidence of SIDS in the UK was approximately 1 in 8,500 in middle-class families with no known risk factors;
  • Meadows assumed the two death were independent and just squared that.

Any problems?

The prosecutor’s fallacy

Even if you accept the number (which you shouldn’t):

saying that there is a 1 in 73 million chance that the babies died of SIDS is not the same as saying that there is a 1 in 73 million chance that the mother did not kill them.

\[ P(evidence \mid innocence) \neq P(innocence\mid evidence). \]

The prosecutor’s fallacy

A sad story

  • 1999: Clark convicted.
  • Stats community loudly panned Meadow’s testimony;
  • 2003: conviction overturned on appeal after revelation that exculpatory evidence was suppressed;
  • 2007: Clark drinks herself to death.

The Daniela Poggiali case

Background

  • Daniela Poggiali (DP) was a hospital nurse accused of murdering a 78-year-old patient by injecting her with a lethal dose of potassium chloride;
  • DP was known to have complained about the patient, and there was an incident of some KCl going missing, but otherwise no one witnessed anything;
  • Data was available on hospital shift assignments and patient mortality;
  • Prosecution argued that a disproportionate number of patients died while DP was on duty;
  • The jury was convinced this could not be mere chance, and DP was convicted.
